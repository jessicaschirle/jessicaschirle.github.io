Math Resources

Nonmath or Nonresource

    Advice & Exposition

  • How to Solve It, by Georg Pólya
    • This is the best book on problem-solving, mathematical or otherwise.
      Required reading for any student.
  • Terry Tao's blog
    • A blog about all things mathematics. Career advice. Writing advice. Research advice.
  • 3Blue1Brown
    • One of the best math YouTubers. Clean exposition and stunningly beautiful graphics. And, as a note to educators, his FAQ says that one is free to use the visuals offline for classroom usage.
  • Quanta Magazine
    • One of the best magazines for reporting on progress in mathematics research (and other scientific research in general). Generally tries to avoid hype in science.

    • References & Research

  • Map of the Universe - Forking and Dividing
    • A model theorist's map of common theories and their placement in the universe.
  • The Model Theory Wiki
    • Model theorists previously had a wiki on fandom.com. This was a helpful community resource for many years, but c. 2022, an unknown user began contributing spam pages featuring the other kind of model (and entrepreneurs, for some reason). James Hanson has placed what was recoverable from the Internet Archive onto his website as a temporary measure.
  • Groupprops Wiki
    • Groupprops is (or was?) a wiki hosting, as expected, a lot of information about groups, including definitions, facts, and plenty of examples of groups, including their subgroups, elements, and representations.
  • π-Base
    • Counterexamples in Topology. Inspired by a book of the same name.
  • nLab
    • A wiki reference for work on math, physics, and philosophy, typically from the point of view of category theory and related fields.
  • Math StackExchange / MathOverflow
    • StackExchange began as a site for programmers to freely ask and answer questions. Now, Math StackExchange exists for the same reason, albeit geared towards math questions at an undergraduate level. This is a helpful resource for anyone pursuing a B.S. in math. MathOverflow, on the other hand, is geared more towards research-based questions in mathematics.
  • arXiv
    • Open-access pre-preprints of scientific articles. These are not peer-reviewed, and so it is good form to reference only those articles which have been published in established journals.
  • Math Genealogy Project
    • The family tree of mathematicians where the parent-child relationship is instead an advisor-advisee relationship. This terminology leads to some using the phrase "academic brother/sister/sibling" or "academic grandparent."

    • All about LaTeX

  • Getting Started with LaTeX
    • Mathematicians use LaTeX (pronounced [LAH-tek] /ˈlɑːtɛk/ or [LAY-tek] /ˈleɪtɛk/, but never [LAY-teks]) to typeset papers with clean formulas and diagrams. This provides several different ways to get started with an installation of LaTeX.
  • Quiver
    • A helpful online tool for generating TikZ-cd code for commutative diagrams.
  • DeTeXify
    • This should be in every mathematicians bookmarks bar. Draw a symbol and get the corresponding LaTeX code.
  • Taking Notes during Lecture with LaTeX
    • Gilles Castel was a mathematics Ph.D. student at KU Leuven until he passed in 2022. His website contains four blog posts that instantly solidified him as a master of LaTeX and note taking. He has many TeXed lecture notes that were written live during the lectures he took.
  • TeX for Gmail
    • A helpful Google Chrome extension that allows one to type LaTeX into an email and compile the code prior to sending. This is most helpful when emailing students.
  • TikZiT
    • A program for generating TikZ code for more general diagrams than just commutative ones.

    Webcomics

  • xkcd
    • A very popular webcomic written by a former NASA engineer. xkcd is known for its nerdiness, commentary, and longevity. It has been updated consistently since 2005. "xkcd" is not an initialism.
  • Spiked Math
    • A webcomic written by someone while in a math Ph.D. program. Much more mathematically focused than xkcd.
  • Abstruse Goose
    • Another nerdy webcomic. This went offline in 2023, but there is now an archive on github thanks to dedicated community members.
  • SMBC
    • Another nerdy webcomic. The linked comic holds a lot of truth to it. "SMBC" is an initialism for "Saturday Morning Breakfast Cereal."

    • Other

  • Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy
    • Exactly what it sounds like. There's also a great deal about logic in the SEP.
  • A Friendly Introduction to the Riemann Hypothesis
    • A humorous introduction to the Riemann Hypothesis. True history mixed with humor is sometimes a dangerous combination, but there is quite a bit of good math in here. The only mathematical error I was able to spot was not writing "..." after the imaginary part of each zero for the Riemann zeta function. That is, towards the end of page 13, it ought to read that ζ(½+14.134725142... i)=0, and similarly for the other three zeros.
  • Unclobbering the Tangled Mess
    • A nuanced look into accepting LGBT people in the Christian church.
  • Pi World Ranking List
    • A list of people of have taken the time to memorize many digits of pi.
